Thoughts and Lessons Learned

I learned the term biodeutsch, defined as "non-immigrant-background German", when I commented to my friend in Berlin that there were just a lot of people who looked German. He responded by teaching me the term and telling me to wait until I see what Heidelberg looks like. It did not disappoint. There were German-looking people all over. What constitutes a German-looking person? I don't have a good answer, and I'm sure I was confounded by being in Germany and knowing these people were German.

There were so many tourists in Heidelberg. Chinese, Korean, Russian, French, Dutch, Danes, and Indians (and probably others I'm forgetting). A few things here:

  • Why were so many freakin' tourists in Heidelberg, a seemingly random small German town. Was the giant wine barrel that well-known? Did the small-town reputation draw in crowds from all over (see Luckenbach, Texas)? Did I just show up on a random day where a bunch of tourists showed up?
  • My friend's mom has a joke that there are actually 200 million Dutch people, but 180 million of them are on vacation at any given time.
  • It's amazing to me how many tourists exist in the world alone. If I was just in Heidelberg and saw that many, imagine how many were in Berlin, Munich, Frankfurt, etc. And that's just Germany! And that's just Europe! Data shows there were 969 million tourists in 2022.

The smalltowniness of Heidelberg was palpable. The streets were narrow and lined with cafe tables and chairs; cyclists casually rode alongside cars, baskets full of groceries; children frolicked in the streets with their parents (and trick-or-treated on Halloween); people waved at each other with airs of familiarity and love. It was a beautiful feeling and sight I had never seen before. A few more things here:

  • I wonder how much of the neighborly feelings I saw were a "we both live in a small town and are proud of it, so let's be friendly and act like small towners" vs. actually knowing each other. While I've seen proud city-ers before, it just wasn't the same.
  • While I never read it, Jane Jacobs' views on mixed-use development and walkability is almost certainly "right". Apartments next to businesses next to parks next to homes, safe sidewalks with cars going a safe speed through the streets. Is this happiness? Are suburbs misery? It sure felt and feels like it sometimes.
  • I'd be curious to see what patterns from A Pattern Language are present.
