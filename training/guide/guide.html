Training Guide

Note: This is out of date as of 08 September 2020. I will update in December 2020 to reflect my current philosophy and include actual literature to support my arguments.

Note that the "guide" part of this essay focuses mostly on strength and recovery. I do not include how to develop or train flexibility, power, or other physical skills as listed in Crossfit's definition of fitness.

Definition and Measurement

Crossfit answered this question with an article back in 2002, titled What Is Fitness?. Their answer consisted of three parts, all of which I think do an excellent job of providing a comprehensive definition of fitness:

  1. Physical skills: Cardiovascular endurance, stamina, strength, flexibility, power, speed, coordination, agility, balance and accuracy. The first four are developed through training (exercise), the last four are developed through practice, and the middle two (power, speed) are developed through a mixture of both.
  2. Generality and Random Tasks: The first part of Crossfit's definition gives the concept of generality. Rather than being good at one or two domains of fitness—say endurance and flexibility—one's training and practice should strive to develop all domains. If you can lift a lot in a few movements, but can't touch your toes or run a mile, you're not fit, you're strong. The example they use in their article is a hopper filled with random physical tasks, e.g., handstand walk, half-marathon run, long jump, etc. The number of tasks you can perform out of the available tasks is a direct measurement of your fitness.
  3. Energy Pathways: There are three pathways that provides us energy: phosphogen (high-power), anaerobic (moderate-power), and aerobic (low-power).

Fitness measurement is also relative. Unless you're Mat Fraser (who is arguably the fittest person on Earth, despite protestations) or another professional Crossfitter, you are not and likely will never be the fittest. Instead, fitness should be measured relative to a few things:

  1. Gender and age group. How fit are you compared to others in your age-gender group? A teenager is expected to be weaker than a 20-something-year-old, making for an unfair comparison.
  2. Training history. How long have you been training for? Has it been consistent? Has it been strictly X discipline, such as powerlifting, or have you changed over the years? I tend to jump around on programs quite a bit, and this has caused me significant lack of progress over the years. From ages 13-15 I focused on running and dabbled in calisthenics, then switched to calisthenics and weights from 16-21, and am currently (07 April 2020) running a barbell-focused program (see Tactical Barbell). Over these years, my training has been quite inconsistent: I train for X months, stop for Y months, train for Z months. This leads to poor fitness compared to my expected level had I trained consistently throughout the years.
  3. Your former self. Cliché, but true nonetheless. If you are stronger than you were a year ago, that's a good sign. If you're equally as strong as yourself a year ago, then you're likely doing something wrong and should reevaluate your training. Keep in mind that your one-rep max (1RM) is not the sole measurement of strength - it is one method, but not the only one.

Philosophy

My training philosophy is simple and quite similar to Crossfit's (with some parts cut out). Your body should be ready for anything that you throw at it (with limits imposed, of course). If you need to throw a ruck on and march a marathon on a whim or squat X plates for a set of 10, you should be able to do it. This is what some people refer to as "operator fitness". Mountain Tactical Institute sums it up well:

  • High relative strength. While staying lean, you should be able to lift a significant factor of your bodyweight (BW) on most lifts.
  • High work capacity. Maintaining a high level of intensity for an extended period of time (up to 30 min).
  • Endurance. Running, rucking, and other aerobic activities.

To summarize the above points: you're strong, fast, and can sustain long periods of both low- and moderate-intensity exercise.

Going about achieving this type of fitness should be done with as little time spent training as possible. A bit odd to hear, as most people correlate time spent training with increases in strength. The curve is parabolic with the sweet spot being right in the middle.

Exercise

Strength

Program Design

The only way to get stronger is to use progressively increasing resistance (referred to as the progressive overload principle). The resistance can be in the form of external weights (barbell, dumbbell, etc.) or using your body's leverage.

You must work in the rep ranges and rest times that meet your goals. The strength range of 1-6 is optimal. It contributes to endurance (if you can bench 315 lb for reps, 100 lb is easy, but the converse is not necessarily true) and hypertrophy, provided you eat enough (see the recovery section).

You must be consistent. Everyone has obligations, so missing a session isn't the end of the world. It's when you begin missing 2 out of 3 sessions per week, then only making 3 out of 6 sessions per two weeks, and so on. Consistency will win every time.

1RMs and training to failure should rarely be performed. They are immensely taxing on your CNS and cause more harm than good. Instead, work in the submaximal 3-5 rep range and estimate using a 1RM calculator.

You can get strong through submaximal lifting. One part of getting stronger is developing the movement patterns and teaching your body to perform it more efficiently. Even if you can squat 300 lb, squatting 225 lb will still be beneficial for helping to develop the movement patterns. The other way to get stronger is to gain muscle, as force (strength) is proportional to the physiological cross-sectional area.

Progression of weight or reps should be slow. This builds a solid foundation of strength and ensures muscles/tendons/ligaments have enough time to adapt to the new load. Patience is key. Trying to add 5-10 lb/week is unsustainable and will quickly overwhelm your body.

Form should be focused on and developed, but not at the cost of full range-of-motion (ROM) and strength. Having a massive arch during bench press may be considered better form for powerlifting, but ROM is sacrificed. An exercise set should be stopped when the form becomes dangerous, not necessarily bad—there is a difference.

Rest

Rest when you feel like you need it. You have years and years to train, and taking a day or two off to let your body recover will not be a detriment. If anything, it will be helpful.

Take a week or two completely off once or twice per year. This week helps your body recuperate after months of hard training. Basic walking and stretching are encouraged during this period.

Deload at least once every 8 weeks. See more information about deloading here. I normally cut both volume and intensity slightly.

Recovery

Nutrition

Caloric intake is king when it comes to gaining or losing weight. It is impossible to gain weight when on a caloric deficit (calories in < calories out) and impossible to lose weight on a caloric surplus (calories in > calories out). If you want to gain strength, a caloric surplus or eating right at maintenance (calories in ~ calories out) is your best bet, as it's difficult to gain strength in a deficit.

Thousands of books have been written on nutrition. Some demonize certain foods, others say you can't [get strong/lose weight/be healthy] without doing this, and so on. If you are unfamiliar with this topic, it can be easy to get lost in the sheer amount of information that is told to you by Dr. Snake-Oil. Diet should not be that difficult or complicated. Keep it simple with the following:

  • Eat lots of meat and vegetables.
  • Eat some fruits and nuts.
  • Eat minimal processed food. These foods are found in the middle of the grocery store.
  • Drink only water and no- or low-calorie drinks (tea, coffee). Sugary drinks (soda, sports, juices) and alcohol are not healthy.

Crossfit and Mark Twight are big fans of Dr. Barry Sears' Zone Diet (Zone Meal Plans here). I've yet to read his book, but will post a review here when I do.

Rest

You must get high-quality sleep. Note I did not include anything about quantity. While this does matter (no one has survived on 0 hours of sleep), it is secondary to the quality. Sleep hygiene is the main contributor of ensuring you get high-quality sleep. The "Quick Sleep Tips" on the page above are all great, especially three points:

  • Keep a consistent sleep schedule: wake up and go to bed around the same time every morning and evening.
  • Only associate your bed with what you want to use it for: sleep and sex. This teaches your body that when you climb into bed, you are going to sleep. Reading or meditating are also fine to do right in bed to relax the body.
  • Minimize lights during maximum darkness. This includes alarm clocks, electronics (especially blue light, I highly suggest installing a warm light app on your phone and computer if your OS doesn't have a feature), and home or street lights. Blackout curtains are a great way of blocking light entering through windows, as well as trapping and blocking heat when needed (winter and summer).

You must treat rest days as rest days. Basic exercise to get the blood flowing, like walking or stretching, is fine, but nothing more intense than that. After all, "recovery is more than 50 percent of the process." (I suggest reading the entire article.)

Standards

All weight standards should be set in factors of BW, not absolute weight. Other standards, such as rowing, favor certain body types, but are by no means unattainable for the devoted athlete. Both endurance and strength are combined in each bullet point. Notice there is no 1RM. Once a standard (whether yours or mine) is met, it should be put on maintenance and your focus turned to other standards not yet achieved. For example, if you have 5 reps of 2xBW BS while back squatting 3x per week, switch to 2x front squat and 1x back squat per week. This will maintain your BS strength while increasing your FS strength.

More good standards by Rob MacDonald here.

Format is:

  • Lift: [reps]x [factor]x[BW]. If BW is 150 lb, then back squat: 5x 2xBW = 5 reps x 300 lb.
  • Strength range is the first, larger weight and endurance is the second, smaller weight (after the "+" sign).

Lower body:

  • Back squat: 5x 2xBW + 25x 1xBW
  • Front squat: 5x 1.5xBW + 25x 0.75xBW
  • Deadlift: 5x 2.5xBW + 25x 1.5xBW

Upper body (1xBW is added weight, 0xBW is no weight added):

  • Chin-/pull-up: 5x 0.5xBW + 25x 0xBW
  • Dip: 5x 1xBW + 50x 0xBW
  • Overhead press: 5x 1xBW + 25x 0.5xBW

Endurance:

  • Running:
    • 800 m: Sub-2:30 min
    • 1600 m: Sub-6:00 min
    • 5000 m: Sub-21:00 min
    • Half-marathon: Sub-1:45:00 min
  • Rowing:
    • 500 m: Sub-1:30 min
    • 2000 m: Sub-7:00 min

Flexibility:

  • Standing pike: Palms-to-floor
  • Third-world squat: 3:00 min hold (barefoot on flat ground)
  • Shoulder flexion: 180° (no external assistance)
  • Shoulder extension: 150-170° German Hang

Resources

Below are a list of resources for getting started with training. They are all reputable and proven to work. As mentioned in the Program Design section, consistency is key. Stick with one program for at least six months, preferably longer, before you change programs. Don't bastardize it. They are well-known for a reason: they work as prescribed!

  • Starting Strength (Amazon here). Mark Rippetoe's most famous book, geared primarily towards beginners starting barbell strength training.
  • StrongLifts 5x5. Basic, straightforward, and effective.
  • Tactical Barbell I (strength) and II (conditioning). My favorite for its ability to design the program for life and its adherence to my training philosophy. Great communities on both r/tacticalbarbell and Tactical Barbell forum.
  • Overcoming Gravity: A Systematic Approach to Gymnastics and Bodyweight Strength by Steven Low. Definitive guide to bodyweight strength training and programming. Great communities on r/overcominggravity.
  • Gym Jones Training Plans. While I haven't actually used any myself, their reputation is stellar.
  • Other programs. Includes 5/3/1, Greyskull LP, nSuns, etc.
