Background

Almost all food labels are attractive in some way: the font, color, description, shape. We're manipulated by the food advertising version of the Halo effect and yet we never realize it when purchasing.

What if labels were made both as boring and grotesque as possible to discourage consumers from buying said products?

An Attractive Agglomeration of Adjectives

Food and brand names are often euphonious (although it can be difficult to decouple what sounds good to the ear and what tastes good to the mouth): Chips Ahoy!, Gushers, Aunt Jemima syrup, Oreo, Reese's, Hershey's, Doritos, Cheetos, M&Ms, Little Debbie. Name a candy or snack food and it probably has a ring to it.

The flavors and types sound equally as pleasant:

  • Fruit Roll-Ups: Blue Razzberry, Strawberry Blast, Tropical Tie Dye
  • Little Debbie: Twinkies, Fudge Rounds, Cosmic Brownies, Oatmeal Creme Pie
  • Mike and Ike: Tropical Typhoon, Berry Blast, Red Rageous

Examples of Egregious Effects

The FDA requires cigarette packages to be labeled with health warnings in order to inform or remind users of the negative health consequences of smoking.

Strong et al.'s Effect of Graphic Warning Labels on Cigarette Packs on US Smokers’ Cognitions and Smoking Behavior After 3 Months found the following:

Health concerns increased in all groups, with a significant increase in the GWL [graphic warning label] group vs the US pack group [just a standard U.S. cigarette pack with no GWL]

But the ultimate result was no change in smoking behavior:

At 3 months, there was no between-group difference in any smoking behavior. The blank pack group was similar to the US pack group on all measures.

With a small note that the finding "may enhance other tobacco control strategies to reduce cigarette smoking".

Clark et al.'s Impact of health warning labels on snack selection: An online experimental study looked at both text-based and image-based warnings and found that:

Health warning labels – particularly those including an image and text - have the potential to reduce selection of energy-dense snacks in an online setting. Their impact on selection and consumption in real-world settings awaits testing.

(It would be challenging to get a company to agree to real-world testing on their foods.)
