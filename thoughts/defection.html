Theory

Brian Whitworth's Social Defection sums up the concept well:

Every social synergy has a corresponding defection, e.g. in trade, when sellers can defect by false advertising, shoddy products or bad warranties, buyers buy less. Buyers can also defect, e.g. buy an expensive ball gown, wear it to a ball, then falsely request a refund, saying it did not fit. If many customers do that, sellers offer less, e.g. refuse refunds (also defect), even though refunds benefit both seller (more sales) and buyer (less risk).
On a personal level it always pays to defect ... The idea that one can get something for nothing is the myth of our generation. All crime is essentially socially unsustainable.
As more people defect, this increases the pressure on the remainder to also defect, etc. Hence a common reason given for cheating is that “everyone is doing it” (Callahan, 2004). A few defections can cause a chain reaction that destabilizes an entire social system.

Defection is acceptable in some cases where others aren't harmed, others are unlikely to defect in the same manner, and others won't get motivated to do it.

Punishment

Defectors should be punished publicly, loudly, swiftly, and harshly for their wrongdoings. Stand up for what is right and defy what is wrong. Show forgiveness to accidental transgressions, but do not tolerate intentional violations.

Examples

Legal and illegal acts written in the form of defection.

  • Transit: Riding a public transit system and not purchasing a ticket
  • Voting: Not voting in elections
  • Littering
  • Parking: Illegitimately parking in a visitor spot (i.e., using it for something other than visiting that parking space's "owner"); parking in a manner that holds up traffic to quickly get something done; parking the wrong way on the street
  • Driving: Cutting through parking lots to avoid the traffic light or long turn line; driving through a red light even when's it's objectively safe
  • Dinner: Saying it's someone's birthday at a restaurant when it's not
  • Access: Taking not-on-the-list strangers into an exclusive location; telling a company a false story to get order access to a restricted part
  • Trash: Not following trash rules for bins or chutes
  • Returns: Buying something, wearing/using it once, and returning it; telling a restaurant the food was bad to get a refund; not putting shopping carts back in their corral
  • Payment: Choosing an expensive restaurant/ordering extra food when someone else is paying (this is also called being a douchebag)
  • Cheating: All types of games; doping in sport; academic settings
  • Social loafing: "Exerting less effort to achieve a goal when they work in a group than when working alone"
  • Reservations: Reserving something with a lenient cancellation policy on the off-chance it will be used
  • Noise: Playing music out loud or talking on the phone on public transit
  • Punctuality: Not showing up on time
  • Animals: Letting dogs off leash; not picking up dog poop; allowing constantly-barking dogs in the backyard
