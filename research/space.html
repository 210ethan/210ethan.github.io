Space: How Do Objects Collide?

After reading about Kessler syndrome and The Elusive Peril of Space Junk, my first thought was "how the heck do objects in orbit collide?". There's so much space (no pun intended) up there!

Background

First, orbitals need to be understood, as they are not perfectly circular about an axis (planet).

Working Area

The most simple model of the orbit is the surface area of a sphere, where the radius is the radius of Earth plus the distance above Earth's surface the objects orbit it. The mean radius of Earth is 6371 km and the International Space Station (ISS) orbits at around 400 km above Earth's surface. This results in a working surface area of:

\[\begin{align*} \text{SA} &= 4 \pi \left(r_{\text{earth}} + r_{\text{orbit-surface}}\right)^{2} \\ &= 4 \pi \left(6371 + 400\right)^{2} \\ &= 5.76 \times 10^{8} \, \text{km}^{2} \\ \end{align*} \]

This model assumes point-like objects that can move two-dimensionally throughout the surface areaâ€”they cannot move towards or away from Earth.

The issue with this is differing orbits. The ISS orbits in low Earth orbit, defined as 0-2000 km above the Earth's surface. There are a few other types of orbits:

  • Low Earth (LEO): 0-2000 km
  • Medium Earth (MEO): 2,000-35,786 km
  • Geosynchronous and geostationary (GSO, GEO): 35,786 km
  • High Earth (HEO): >35,786 km

Using these ranges, a shell of permissible space can be found, assuming the lowest possible orbit altitude is 200 km and the highest is :

\[\begin{align*} V_{\text{shell}} &= \frac{4}{3} \pi \left(r_{\text{HEO}} - r_{\text{LEO}}\right) \\ &= \frac{4}{3} \pi \left(xxxxx - 200\right) \\ &= \, \text{km}^{3} \end{align*} \]
