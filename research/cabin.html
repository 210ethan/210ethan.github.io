Background

M. Night Shyamalan's Knock at the Cabin tells the story of a family—two men and their adopted daughter—meeting a polite, yet insistent, group of strangers who come to the family's cabin claiming the world will end if they don't sacrifice one of themselves. The strangers know this because of a vision they all had at the same time.

The following events happen after the strangers sacrifice one of their own to further delay the impending doom:

  • Tsunamis
  • Disease (in the form of the flu)
  • Plane crashes

The whole "you wouldn't do X if your friend told you to" spiel my parents would tell me when I was young was, and still is, bullshit. What if I replaced "your friend told you to" with "multiple highly improbable events occurred"? You bet your ass I'd follow the lemmings and expect you to do the same.

This essay will help me decide if I should sacrifice myself if I'm ever in the position of that poor family.

Calculations

First, a short primer on Bayes' theorem. The Bayes' rule: Guide is Yudkowsky-approved and a good summary with multiple difficulty levels. Bayes' theorem states that:

\[P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \times P(A)}{P(B)}\]

where:

  • \(P(A|B)\) is the probability of \(A\) given \(B\) is true, and vice versa for B given A
  • \(P(A)\), \(P(B)\) is the probability of A and B

In other words, if the ones of the right-hand probabilities change (priors), then the left-hand probability, \(P(A|B)\) or the posterior, should also change. This is referred to as updating.

The question is: what is the probability of an apocalypse happening given tsunamis, disease, and plane crashes are occurring?

This can be formulated as:

P(A | T, D, C) = P(T, D, C | A) × P(A) ÷ P(T, D, C)

We can further break it up to individual probabilities:

P(A | T, D, C) = P(T | A) × P(D | A) × P(C | A) × P(A) ÷ (P(T) × P(D) × P(C))

Tsunamis: P(T | A) and P(T)

What is the probability of massive tsunamis given an apocalyse is occurring?

Probably pretty high. Remember, an apocalypse is "the complete final destruction of the world" or "an event involving destruction or damage on a catastrophic scale".

I'll assign P(T | A) a value of 0.6, or 60%:

P(T | A) = 0.6

According to U.S. Tsunami Warning Center, damaging tsunamis occur about twice per decade. That's two in 3,650 days, a value of 0.00005, or 0.005% chance of a tsunami occurring on any given day.

But we can further fine-tune this value given the information that an 8.6-magnitude earthquake, originating from the Cascadia Subduction Zone, hit the Oregon coast. Oregon State University concluded there is "more than a one-in-three chance that [a >8.0-magnitude earthquake] will happen within the next 50 years". If we assume the probability is uniform across time, it becomes a 0.00002027, or 0.002027% chance, a massive tsunami happens on any given day.

Thus, the probability of either of these happening on a given day is 0.00007, or 0.007%:

P(T) = 0.00007
