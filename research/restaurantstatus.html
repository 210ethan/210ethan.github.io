Thesis

People who claim to enjoy high-end restaurants because of the food rarely have the palate to distinguish the expensive meal from one at a medium-end restaurant, and, in some cases, low-end. Rather, their liking the restaurant is almost always a simple, not-frowned-upon method of signaling status to peers, regardless of whether or not they belong to the group they're trying to belong to. (Others reasons may include the atmosphere, service, uniqueness of the dishes offered, etc., but these are minor when compared to status.)

I perform a literature review and run experiments.

What's the motivation behind this? Why can't I just let people pay the money they want to eat where they want? The reason is two-fold:

  1. Because I paid $140 for an extremely meh meal that I probably could've gotten for $100 less at any ol' strip mall joint. I want to see if people in general, including myself, can tell the difference between good and bad, so in the future I can choose restaurants accordingly. Why should I pay extra money for zero extra enjoyment. The status gains are beneath me.
  2. Because I want to expose the people for who they are: status chasers. Just come out and say it. Saying food is soooooo good just because of the name (and then finding out it isn't actually from said restaurant) is the adult equivalent of a teenager unknowingly drinking non-alcoholic beer and acting drunk.

Origins of High-Status Restaurants

This prompts a few questions. Is the food at these high-status restaurants really that good and my palate just that bad? Is it people pretending to think it's good because it would be considered low status to not? And if yes, how did the restaurant originally become high status, especially newer ones?

Exclusivity

Exclusivity of a restaurant is primarily determined by wealth via menu prices: the higher the price, the fewer the people who choose to afford it. If a restaurant sets its prices high immediately upon opening, patrons will assume it's good because of the price (because nothing can taste bad if the restaurant has a $$$ or $$$$ rating on Google!).

Name Recognition

Name recognition is perhaps a subcategory of exclusivity, and goes with ingredient names (see truffles, caviar), chef names (see Gordon Ramsay, Wolfgang Puck), and restaurant brand (see Nobu, Capital Grille, Truluck's, etc.)

Experiment 1: Blind Tasting

Have participants blind-taste food from high-status and low-status restaurants without knowing the source. Ask them to rate the food on taste, presentation, and overall satisfaction.

Participants are blindfolded and given food from either a high-end or low-end restaurant—they know the food type and nothing else about anything.

Experiment 2: Price Information Manipulation

Serve the same dish to two groups: one group is told it’s from a high-end restaurant, and the other is told it’s from a low-cost eatery. Compare their ratings to see if perceived price influences their evaluation.
