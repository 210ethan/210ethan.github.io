Vacuum: Theory and Technologies

What is Pressure?

Pressure is defined by the simple equation:

\[P = \frac{F}{A}\]

where \(P\) is pressure, \(F\) is force, and \(A\) is the area the force is exerted on. A common experiment to show this equation in-action is laying on a bed of nails: if \(F\) (the person's body) stays constant and \(A\) is large (the area of the bed), the pressure exerted on the skin will be minimized, preventing penetration. If it's a single nail on the other hand...

Now that pressure has been shown with an actual body (literally!) of mass, what about pressure that is invisible, e.g., air?

From the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution, which is used to find the speeds of gases, the diatomic N2 molecule moves at:

\[v_{\text{N}_{2}} = \sqrt{\frac{2k_{B}T}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(1.380 \times 10^{-23} \, \text{J}\cdot\text{K}^{-1})(273 \, \text{K})}{2 \times 2.33 \times 10^{-26} \, \text{kg}}} \approx 400 \, \text{m/s}\]

Quite fast! If a human got by a car going the same speed (900 mph or 1500 kph), they would surely die. The difference is the size of the object. The N2 molecule has an insignificant mass of \(4.6 \times 10^{26}\) kg, resulting in a insignificant kinetic energy and not noticing its collisions. However, the number density of gases is extremely high, leading to many collisions and the experience of pressure.

Pressure has many units:

  • Torr (symbol: Torr or mmHg, millimeters of mercury): Named after Evangelista Torricelli and his barometer experiments, 1 Torr is the amount of pressure needed to support a 1 mm-tall column of Hg at 0 Â°C and standard gravity. 760 Torr is the standard atmospheric pressure at sea-level. Note that "torr" is the unit name and "Torr" is the symbol.
  • Standard atmosphere (symbol: atm): Pressure at sea-level. 1 atm = 101.325 Pa = 14.7 psi = 760 Torr.
  • Bar (symbol: bar): 1 bar = 100,000 Pa.
  • Pascal (symbol: Pa): The derived SI unit for pressure: \[1 \, \text{Pa} = 1 \, \frac{\text{N}}{\text{m}^{2}}\]
  • Pounds per square inch (symbol: psi): Freedom units of pressure.

What is Vacuum?

Vacuum is formally defined as "a space entirely devoid of matter." In regards to practical definitions, it is a space that has a lower pressure (or lower amount of particles) than its surroundings, all other variables kept equal.

Achieving Vacuum

Vacuum is achieved through the use of vacuum pumps.
